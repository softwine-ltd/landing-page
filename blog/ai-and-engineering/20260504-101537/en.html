AI Didn't Replace the Engineer - It Replaced the Question

For years, every CTO I've known has wrestled with the same classic dilemma: build or buy. Develop an internal solution or approve a budget for an off-the-shelf tool. Pay in engineering resources or pay in dollars.

AI buried that dilemma.

Not because it solved it - because it made it irrelevant. When any developer on your team can tell their army of bots to spin up a custom solution by lunchtime, "build or buy" sounds like a question from a different era.

And from a business perspective, that's a dream. Why would I approve a budget for yet another shelf tool when my team can produce something tailored exactly to our needs?

But dreams, as we know, can turn into nightmares.

The Bots Build Fast, but They Make Architectural Decisions in the Dark

The pace is dizzying. New tools go live in days instead of weeks. Every developer on the team is now a foreman overseeing an army of code agents. The ROI looks fantastic - on paper.

But here's where the real reckoning begins.

These bots don't understand business context. They don't know your system needs to survive Black Friday traffic spikes. They don't think about backward compatibility, or what happens when your biggest customer tries something nobody planned for. They just build - fast, confident, and without asking questions.

That's exactly the problem.

If you're not paying attention, you end up with a black box bursting with patches. Layers of code nobody on the team truly understands, architectural decisions made silently without anyone signing off on them, and dependencies that only surface when something breaks in production on a Friday night.

"Time to market" - that's sometimes a polished way of saying you pushed something to production without having a clue how it's built.

Technical Debt Wearing a Speed Costume

At the business level, what looks like savings is actually technical debt waiting to explode. And this is a new breed of debt - because with regular technical debt, at least someone on the team understands what shortcuts were taken. With AI-generated debt, nobody necessarily knows where the time bombs are buried.

Think of it this way: when a human developer writes quick-and-dirty code, they at least know where they cut corners. They can go back and fix it. When a bot writes code, it doesn't cut corners - it simply doesn't know corners exist. Its architectural decisions look reasonable at the function level, but at the system level they can create an unmaintainable monster.

The business risk is real. Every week that passes without someone reviewing the architecture the AI built, the cost of fixing it grows. Not linearly - exponentially. Because every new component built on shaky foundations deepens the problem.

The War Room Moved to Developer Communities

So what do you do? How do you maintain architectural ownership in a world where code gets written faster than anyone can read it?

The answer I've found isn't technological - it's communal. Our war room moved to developer communities on WhatsApp and Discord, and a bit on LinkedIn. That's where we tear apart the code, question the "magic" of AI, and make sure we're not losing ownership of the architecture.

In practice, that means every solution a bot produces goes through human review - not just at the code level, but at the decision level. Why this architecture? What happens with ten thousand concurrent users? Where are the failure points? These are questions bots never ask themselves, so someone has to ask on their behalf.

And that brings us back to the core principle: think twice, build once. Even when AI lets you build ten times over, that doesn't mean you should. Sometimes this very speed demands that we slow down and think more, not less.

An Insanely Fast Contractor - but Not a Structural Engineer

The most accurate metaphor I've found for AI's role on a dev team is the difference between a building contractor and a structural engineer.

An insanely fast contractor is great for ROI. They lay bricks quickly, plaster walls efficiently, and finish ahead of schedule. But the structural engineer's job - deciding where the load-bearing columns go, how thick the foundations need to be, and how the building survives an earthquake - that has to stay with us.

The problem is that the line between the two is blurring. When a bot writes code, it isn't just executing - it's also making design decisions. Every line of code is a small architectural choice, and when thousands of those choices are made without oversight, the result is a building that looks solid from the outside but trembles on the inside.

That's why the CTO's role in the AI era has changed. Less writing code, less managing projects - and more guarding architectural integrity. Being the one who understands the big picture and ensures all the fast-moving pieces the bots produce connect into something that actually stands.

Bottom Line

AI didn't change the answer to the build-or-buy dilemma - it changed the question. The question today isn't "build or buy" but "who decides how to build".

Takeaways

AI's speed is real, but speed without architectural ownership is technical debt in disguise. Every bot-generated solution needs review at the decision level, not just the code level - why this architecture, what happens under load, where are the failure points. Your team needs to be bionic, not passive - using AI as an execution tool while keeping the structural engineer role in-house. And precisely because you can build fast, the principle of "think twice, build once" is more relevant today than ever.

Because in the end, a real product isn't what got built fast - it's what's still standing after a year in production.
